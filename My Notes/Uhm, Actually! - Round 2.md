Statements:

1. Generation 1 of Pokémon introduced three starters: Bulbasaur the Grass/Poison type, Squirtle the Water type, and Charmander the Fire type, who evolves into Charmeleon at level 16 and finally Charizard, a powerful Fire/Dragon type with massive wings and an iconic fiery tail.  
      
    Um, actually, Charizard is a Fire/Flying type, despite looking very much like a dragon — only its Mega Evolution Mega Charizard X gains the Dragon typing.  
      
    
2. Wolverine is one of the most iconic X-Men, who’s mutant powers have given him a powerful healing factor, animalistic senses, and his signature adamantium claws — three retractable, indestructible metal blades that extend from both of his hands.  
      
    Um, actually, Wolverine's claws are bone — the adamantium was bonded to them during the Weapon X experiments.  
      
    
3. Scarlett Johansson played Black Widow throughout the MCU's Infinity Saga, debuting as Natasha Romanoff in 2012's "The Avengers" and going on to star in over half a dozen films before finally getting her standalone movie in 2021.  
      
    Um, actually, Black Widow's first MCU appearance was in 2010's "Iron Man 2," where she went undercover as Tony Stark's assistant Natalie Rushman.  
      
    
4. One of the most iconic moments in film history comes at the climax of "The Empire Strikes Back," where Darth Vader corners Luke on Cloud City, severs his hand, and drops the bombshell revelation with the line: "Luke, I am your father."  
      
    Um, actually, the actual line is "No, I am your father." It's one of the most famous misquotes in film history, often cited as an example of the Mandela Effect.  
      
    
5. In 5th edition D&D, scoring a critical hit is one of the most thrilling moments in combat: rolling a natural 20 on an attack roll means you double the total damage you deal, modifiers and all, often turning a glancing blow into a campaign-defining crunch.  
      
    Um, actually, a critical hit in 5e only doubles the damage dice - modifiers are added once, not doubled.  
      
    
6. In the finale of "Avatar: The Last Airbender," Aang struggles with his pacifist beliefs as he prepares to face Fire Lord Ozai. Ultimately, he learns the lost art of energybending from Avatar Roku in a spiritual vision, and uses it to strip Ozai of his firebending entirely.  
      
    Um, actually, Aang learned energy bending from a giant Lion Turtle, not from Avatar Roku.  
      
    
7. In "The Lord of the Rings," Bilbo Baggins's nephew Frodo inherits the One Ring along with Bag End, after Bilbo finally leaves the Shire on the morning after his 111th birthday party.  
      
    Um, actually, Frodo is Bilbo's cousin - first cousin once removed on his mother's side, and second cousin once removed on his father's side.  
      
    
8. Disney's 1942 animated classic "Bambi" tells the touching coming-of-age story of a young white-tailed deer growing up in the forest. Many remember Bambi as the wide-eyed doe whose mother is tragically killed by hunters, and who falls in love with the buck named Faline by the film's end.  
      
    Um, actually, Bambi is a buck - Bambi is male, and Faline is the doe.  
      
    
9. John Carpenter's 1978 slasher "Halloween" introduced the unstoppable Michael Myers, whose blank white mask has become one of horror's most iconic images. Designer Tommy Lee Wallace sculpted the mask from scratch, intentionally featureless to convey Michael's emotional emptiness.  
      
    Um, actually, the mask was a cheap store-bought 1975 Captain Kirk mask of William Shatner - the crew widened the eyes, teased out the hair, and painted it flat white.  
      
    
10. In Greek mythology, Pandora was the first woman, created by Hephaestus on Zeus's orders. She is most famous for receiving a beautiful box that, when she couldn't resist opening it, released all the evils of the world - leaving only Hope trapped inside.  
      
    Um, actually, in the original Greek myth Pandora opened a jar, not a box. The "box" comes from a 16th-century mistranslation.  
      
    
11. Captain Kirk's most quoted line from the original "Star Trek" series, "Beam me up, Scotty," has been parodied countless times over the decades and remains one of television's most enduring catchphrases.  
      
    Um, actually, that exact line was never said in the original "Star Trek" series. Kirk says variations like "Scotty, beam me up" and "Beam us up, Mr. Scott," but never the famous catchphrase verbatim.  
      
    
12. Zaphod Beeblebrox, the ex-President of the Galaxy in Douglas Adams's "The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy," is famously equipped with two heads and two arms - a configuration he claims helps him pilot the Heart of Gold and look effortlessly cool while doing it.  
      
    Um, actually, Zaphod has three arms — he had the third one fitted just below his right one, supposedly to improve his ski-boxing.
