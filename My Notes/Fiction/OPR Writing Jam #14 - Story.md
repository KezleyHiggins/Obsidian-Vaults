
When Sir Quentin had sworn his oaths as a knight, it had seemed so simple. He had squired for years, put his heart and soul into his training, and hung onto every last word his mentors had uttered. Before he’d found a banner to fight under and a master to swear his sword to, his oaths demanded he protect the people from all harm. So he fought bandits, rebellious guild-men, and fallen knights.

To the lord he finally swore his blade, he promised to protect his heirs, battle any and all enemies of the realm, and uphold his justice. He slew traitors, guarded taxmen while they collected their dues, and brought law to the lawless villages.

To the world, he swore an oath to lay waste to any monster that threatened the land. Every knight did this. His first trophy was the skin of three tigers he’d cut down while protecting a caravan of nobles. The second was a wild gryphon that had attacked his camp while he was on a quest for his liege. The victories had become expected.

When he was awarded his pegasus, he swore to the beast to honour him and keep him safe. He tended to the animal personally whenever he could, largely due to the animal’s fiery temper that had left many a stablehand bitten and bruised.  

Now he stood before the gates of the Oldtower, the last man standing of the host that had sallied out to beat back the forces of Havoc. Any other defenders were safely tucked inside the high walls behind him.

The peasant levies with him had not lasted as long as he had hoped. They had fought bravely, mostly, and died honourably. Despite that, Quentin felt the tenets of his first sworn oath come to mind. He had sworn to protect the common folk, and now he stood alone while those who had fought alongside him lay dead.

A large Northman stood before him, a two-handed axe slick with blood, laughing. Quentin would have to cut this man down, then the ten behind him, then the hundred behind them. All alone. The tenets of his second oath told him he must. He was no god, though, nor a wizard. He was a great knight, perhaps the greatest knight, but he could not stop such an overwhelming force alone.

Behind him, his pegasus lay dead. Quentin's heart ached for him. The poor creature had braved this hellscape nobly, enduring arrows and shrapnel from the enemy below without fear. He had never bucked, or screamed. Quentin had sworn to honour him and keep him safe. How could he do either now?

The figure before him spat to the side and took a step forward. "I can see you're not running," he rumbled. "Why is that? Are you so delicate that you can only flee if your horse can take you?"

Quentin had never been a talker. He’d been born with a stutter that made conversation frustrating, if not impossible. His pegasus hadn't cared, though. Oaths could be sworn once; after that, he could be quiet. His resolve bolstered, he gave his sword a sharp slash through the air, cutting through the conversational debt this cretin was trying to force upon him.

"I-if we m-must fight," he said as he stared down the hulking figure, his voice even and harsh despite the stilt in his words. "Th-then fight. D-don't talk."

It worked. His foe pressed forward, his axe hefted into the air to be brought down against Quentin. The attack was too slow, the Northman's footwork too imprecise. This was easy. Sir Quentin danced to the side as the axe's head buried itself into the muddy field beneath their feet.

His dagger left his belt and thrust deep into the man's neck, driving upward until it became stuck. There was no getting the blade back, so he let it go, and the man fell. The rest of the horde began to charge. The roar of their boots became the only thing he could hear.

All he had left was his sword now. His dagger was lost and his shield abandoned who-knows-where sometime after the death of his pegasus. No, he thought, raising the blade and standing ready to keep fighting. I still have my oaths.

He could not save the levies who had sallied out with him, but he could protect those within the walls. He could not protect his liege directly, but he could stop these men. He could not keep his steed safe, but he could honour his sacrifice.

He could not keep all his oaths and live. Yet he must. As a knight, he must do the impossible. He had done it before. He could do it now. He pressed forward, ready to cut down these ten, then the next hundred, then the next thousand.

Well, he would try.
