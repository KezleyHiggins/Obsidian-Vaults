Quentin had never been a talker. He’d been born with a stutter that made conversation frustrating, if not impossible. He endured judgment and ridicule for how he spoke. He saw no future but becoming a knight, judged by actions alone. 

Soon Quentin had sworn his oaths, and it had all seemed so simple. He had squired for years, put his heart and soul into his training, and hung onto every last word his mentors had uttered. Before he’d found a banner to fight under and a master to swear his sword to, his oaths demanded he protect the people from all harm. So he fought bandits, rebellious guilds, and fallen knights.

To his liege, he promised to protect their heirs, battle any and all enemies of the realm, and uphold their justice. He guarded taxmen, slew traitors, and brought law to the lawless.

To the world, he swore an oath to lay waste to any monster that threatened the land. Every knight did this. His first trophy was the skin of three tigers he’d cut down while protecting a flock of nobles. The second was a wild gryphon that had attacked his camp while he was on a quest. The victories had become expected.

When he was awarded his pegasus, he swore to the beast to honour him and keep him safe. He tended to the animal personally whenever he could, largely due to the animal’s fiery temper that had left many a stablehand bitten and bruised.

The pegasus cared only about actions, not words. It made bonding with him easier. Quentin didn't need to win him over with charm or eloquence; he simply needed to care for the beast and show him respect. They found in each other the kind of loyalty that comes only with time and dedication.

Now Quinten stood before the gates of the Oldtower, the last man standing of the host that had sallied out to beat back the forces of Havoc. The high walls behind him hid any other defenders from his gaze, though he felt their eyes on him. He was their only hope.

The peasant levies with him had not lasted as long as he had hoped. They had fought bravely, mostly, and died honourably. He had tried to command them, call tactics and inspire hope for this battle, but the chaos around them was louder than he was. His voice had always failed him, and now it had failed them.

Quentin felt the tenets of his first oath come to mind. He had sworn to protect the common folk, and now he stood alone while those who had fought alongside him lay dead. Did any of them even hear his voice before they died? Did he hear theirs?

A large Northman stood before him, wielding a two-handed axe slick with blood, laughing. Quentin would have to cut this man down. Then the ten behind him. Then the hundred behind them. Alone. 

The tenets of his second oath told him he must. He was no legend, though. He was just a knight, perhaps the greatest knight, but he could not stop such an overwhelming force alone.

Behind him, his pegasus lay dead. Quentin's heart ached for him. The poor creature had braved this hellscape nobly, enduring arrows and shrapnel from the enemy below without fear. He had never bucked or screamed. Quentin had sworn to honour him and keep him safe.  Yet he wasn’t even able to grant him a painless death.

The Northman before him spat to the side and took a step forward. "I can see why you flew that prissy thing around," he sneered. "You can barely stand on your own two legs!"

All his life, speech had been the real battleground for Quentin. It was always something to be avoided. Oaths only needed to be sworn once; after that, he could be quiet. He gave his sword a sharp slash through the air, cutting through the conversational debt this cretin was trying to force upon him.

Quentin stared down the hulking figure, his voice even and harsh despite the stilt in his words. "I-if we m-must fight, th-then fight. D-don't talk."

His foe let out a snarl and rushed forward, his axe hefted into the air to be brought down against Quentin. The attack was too slow, the Northman's footwork too imprecise. This was easy. Quentin danced to the side as the axe's head buried itself into the mud beneath their feet.

Quentin’s dagger left his belt and thrust deep into the man's neck, driving upward until it became stuck. There was no getting the blade back, so he let it go. The man fell. The rest of the horde charged. The roar of their boots became the only thing he could hear.

His dagger spent and his shield long abandoned, all he had left was his sword. A knight without a steed and a commander without an army. No, he thought, raising the blade and standing ready. I still have my oaths.

He could not save the souls who had fallen beside him, but he could protect those within the walls. He could not keep his steed safe, but he could honour his sacrifice. He could not keep all his oaths and live. Yet he must. 

As a knight, he must do the impossible. He had found a way before. He could find a way now. He pressed forward, ready to cut down these ten, then the next hundred, then the next thousand.

Well, he would try.
