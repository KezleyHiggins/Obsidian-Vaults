
# How V&T is different

At time of writing, a game of V&T includes [[Cypher System#Power Shifts|Power Shifts]] and [[Cypher System#Power Stunts|Power Stunts]] as integrated optional rules which are described and recommended by Monte Cooke games. Power Shifts cover PCs feeling superheroic by just being better at something, really giving the feeling of having superpowers with more of a grounded feeling than just GM fiat saying when they were and weren't having their powers applied. 

If a PC tries to do something where a Power Shift reduces a task to zero, they just do it. Or if they don't just do it, they will have a vastly easier time than someone without them. Good stuff.

Power Stunts are a broader rule which allows for powers to feel dynamic and special, with them not just being a spell that has one sole use and it's solely up to GM fiat whether it can be used in other ways. It gives a way for PCs to spend points from their pools in a way that matches the general curve of point spending for tiers and also gives a benchmark that all decisions can be compared to for them to do something.

If a PC has magic and wants to do something that is hyper specific to the story and isn't covered by an ability explicitly, but in fiction it feels obvious they'd be able to attempt it, then we shift over into a Power Stunt and use that as the vector for them to have a task they can attempt.

# Further Desires

As discussed above, [[Cypher System#Power Shifts|Power Shifts]] and [[Cypher System#Power Stunts|Power Stunts]] do a great job of extending what a Cypher System PC is able to do without making anything particularly unwieldy for players. However, it doesn't really do anything to feel like V&T, it just feels like a superhero story. 

**What is an example?** 
Well, right now a superheroes [[Origin]] is *just* story flavour. A PC who has a [[Origin#Psychics|Psychic]] origin to their powers isn't mechanically any different from a hero with an [[Origin#Aliens|Alien]] origin to their powers. As a GM, I can highlight things in fiction, or prompt the players to, or I might say that something only affects a psychics or something is the equivalent of the Alien's kryptonite, but that is true of any game.

I would want for Origin, like [[Cypher System#Power Shifts|Power Shifts]], to be something that someone engages with that gives them something fun to chew on mechanically and also makes them engage with the setting. Right now, these are the origins that I have listed:

- Aliens
- Bizarre Dimensions
- Cosmic Entity / God
- Lost Civilization
- Magic
- Mutants / Breakouts
- Parallel Earths
- Psychics
- Super Technology
- Heroes of the past 

What if Origins were turned on their head and instead of it being framed as something that the player selects that could be the same per campaign, its instead a box that the character fills along with the campaign story

So if the campaign was about being Spidey Villains who are in a Suicide-Squad type situation, there's a list of six origins (it should be more than the amount of players but not huge) saying that you are an alternate universe version of Doctor Octopus, you have xyz in addition to your normal decisions. This way they could have some custom mechanics and also preserve normal decisions.

# Optional Rules

Cypher System has its [own setting](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1KA-SblKAD-nlOyvX0syGwtnJUCP-TlgH/view?usp=drive_link), along with [miscellaneous rules and advice](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#chapter-18-superheroes). These include Power Shifts and Power Stunts.

## Power Shifts

Power shifts are an optional rule in the Cypher System Rulebook that represent many of the exceptional things that superheroes can do, like throwing cars, blasting through brick walls, leaping onto speeding trains, and cobbling together interdimensional gateway devices in a few hours.

Power shifts are like permanent free levels of Effort that are always active. They don’t count toward a character’s maximum Effort use (nor do they count as skills or assets). They simply ease tasks that fall into specific categories, which include (but are not necessarily limited to) the following.

The book recommends that a typical superhero PC gets five power shifts. However, due to the nature of the setting of Vigilantes & Tragedies, the highest for this setting is three. PCs may distribute their Power Shifts however they like, however they may not select the same option more than twice at character creation.

> **Accuracy:** All attack rolls.
> 
> **Dexterity:** Movement, acrobatics, initiative, and Speed defense.
> 
> **Flight:** The character can fly a short distance each round; each additional shift increases this speed (whether the flight comes from a power shift or a character ability) by one range category (long for two shifts, very long for three shifts).
> 
> **Healing:** One extra (one-action) recovery roll per day.
> 
> **Increased Range:** Increases the range of one ability or attack. A touch-range ability (such as Shock) increases to short range, a short-range ability increases to long range, and a long-range ability increases to very long range.
> 
> **Intelligence:** Intellect defense rolls and all knowledge, science, and crafting tasks. Power: Effects of one specific character ability, including damage for that ability (+3 points) if appropriate, but not attack rolls with that ability.
> 
> **Prodigy:** Give up a lower-tier ability to get a higher-tier ability (see the Prodigy Power Shifts box on page 58).
> 
> **Resilience:** Might defense rolls and Armor (+1).
> 
> **Savant:** Two specific skills (other than attacks, defenses, or a special ability), such as history, perception, or persuasion.
> 
> **Single Attack:** Attack rolls and damage (+3 points) for one specific kind of attack, such as pistols, kicks, or Thrust.
> 
> **Strength:** All tasks involving strength, including jumping and dealing damage with melee or thrown attacks (+3 points), but not attack rolls.

## Power Stunts

A power stunt is pushing a superpower beyond its normal limits or using it to do something it normally can't do. Examples:

- A lightning-blaster hero shooting their electricity farther than normal
- A fire-creating hero absorbing fire from a burning building
- A telepathic hero communicating with or understanding a machine
- A teleporter hero traveling to another dimension
- An illusionist hero negating an opponent's invisibility

The [Cypher System Rulebook](https://www.montecookgames.com/store/product/cypher-system-rulebook-2/) explains [Modifying Abilities on the Fly](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#optional-rule-modifying-abilities-using-initial-costs) (419), describing a method of altering the range, area, or other aspects of an Intellect-based ability by spending more Intellect points. In a superhero game, these modifications aren't limited to Intellect-based abilities- it's reasonable that a strong hero could affect a larger area with [Golem Stomp](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-golem-stomp) or an agile hero could disarm more than one opponent using [Advantage to Disadvantage](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-advantage-to-disadvantage). The cost for making these changes works just like modifying an Intellect-based ability. The additional cost uses the same Pool as the ability's normal cost; if an ability doesn't have a cost, the GM should choose an appropriate ability for the points to come from.

- Increasing range costs 1 Pool point per range step increased (immediate to short, short to long, long to very long).
- Increasing duration costs 1 Pool point for one step (one minute to ten minutes, ten minutes to an hour). Durations cannot be increased more than one step in this way. Abilities that last for only an action or a round (such as an Onslaught attack) cannot have their duration increased.

Abilities that don't have a Pool cost, like [Eyes Adjusted](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-eyes-adjusted), can be modified as well. If modifying the range or duration, the GM decides what Pool the point cost is paid from. However, most abilities like this don't have ranges or durations, so modifying them requires a difficult, formidable, or impossible task roll.

Modifying the area or other aspects of an ability is more difficult. Instead of increasing the [Pool](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#pool) point cost, the character decides how they want to modify their ability, and the GM sets a difficulty of the task to successfully modify it, according to the following guidelines:

|Difficulty|Power Stunt|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|4|**Difficult:** Something within the spirit and general idea of the ability, using a self- only ability on another character, or using a single-target ability in a weakened form on two targets.|- Using a self-only ability like [Hover](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-hover) to give another creature the power to fly.<br>- Using [Teleportation](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-teleportation) to go to another dimension instead of somewhere in the same dimension.<br>- Splitting [Frost Touch](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-frost-touch) or [Onslaught](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-onslaught) into hindered attacks against two opponents.|
|7|**Formidable:** Something similar to the description or intent of the ability, but changing its nature, or having a single-target ability affect an area.|- Using [Hover](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-hover) to make an opponent crash into the ceiling.<br>- Using [Shroud of Flame](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-shroud-of-flame) to absorb fire.<br>- Using [Telepathic](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-telepathic) to talk to a machine or [Machine Telepathy](https://callmepartario.github.io/og-csrd/#ability-machine-telepathy) to talk to a living person.|
